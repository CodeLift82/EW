The Elliott Wave Principle
Foundation · Market Psychology · The Two Families
ℹR.N. Elliott discovered in the 1930s that markets move in repetitive fractal patterns reflecting collective human psychology — oscillating between optimism and pessimism, between impulse and correction. Every wave degree, from Grand Supercycle to Subminuette, follows the same 5–3 structure.

🌊 The Two Wave Families

MOTIVE WAVES — move with the larger trend. Always 5 sub-waves. Purpose: generate progress.
Types: Impulse, Leading Diagonal, Ending Diagonal
CORRECTIVE WAVES — move against the larger trend. Always 3 sub-waves (or multiples). Purpose: correct prior progress.
Types: Zigzag, Flat, Triangle, Irregular, Complex combos
IRREGULAR WAVES ★ — Special corrective structures where Wave B exceeds the start of Wave A, creating unexpected extremes before C reversal.
Types: Irregular Flat, Irregular Failure, Running Correction

📐 The 5–3 Golden Rule

Every wave — no matter how large or small — unfolds in this same fractal structure:

5 — 3
MOTIVE (5) + CORRECTIVE (3) = COMPLETE CYCLE (8)
Each motive wave sub-divides into 5 smaller waves
Each corrective wave sub-divides into 3 smaller waves
The 5-wave impulse becomes wave 1 of the next larger degree

Impulse Waves
The Engine of Trend — 5-Wave Motive Structure · All Rules · Extensions · Fibonacci Probabilities
ℹAn impulse wave is the most powerful structure in EW theory — 5 waves in the direction of the trend, each sub-dividing further. Waves 1, 3, and 5 are motive; Waves 2 and 4 are corrective. Three hard rules can never be broken.

🔴 The 3 Unbreakable Rules

RULE 1: Wave 2 NEVER retraces more than 100% of Wave 1. If it does → not Wave 2.
RULE 2: Wave 3 is NEVER the shortest among Waves 1, 3, and 5. W3 must be longer than either W1 or W5.
RULE 3: Wave 4 NEVER enters the price territory of Wave 1 (no overlap). Exception: diagonals only.
✗Any violation of the above rules means the wave count is incorrect. Do not rationalize — recount.

🟡 Guidelines (High Probability)

Wave 3 is most commonly the longest and strongest wave
Wave 2 and Wave 4 tend to alternate in form (Alternation Guideline)
Wave 5 often equals Wave 1 in length, or is a Fibonacci ratio of it
Waves 2 & 4 together tend to take similar time (time equality)
The end of Wave 5 often meets a channel line drawn from Wave 3 top parallel to W1–W2 line
In any impulse, ONE wave will extend — usually W3, sometimes W1 or W5

Corrective Waves
Simple Corrections · Zigzag · Flat · Running Flat

Zigzag (5-3-5)

The sharpest corrective pattern — moves decisively against the prior trend.

Structure: A(5 waves) — B(3 waves) — C(5 waves)
Wave B retraces 38.2%–61.8% of Wave A (never exceeds A start)
Wave C = 0.618–1.618 × Wave A; most commonly 1.0×A
B wave MUST NOT reach or exceed the start of Wave A
Found in: Wave 2, Wave A of larger correction, Wave W of double zigzag
Double Zigzag (WXY): two zigzags connected by an X wave (3 waves)
Zigzag Structure

Regular Flat (3-3-5)

Sideways correction where B ends near the start of A, and C ends near the end of A.

Structure: A(3 waves) — B(3 waves) — C(5 waves)
Wave B retraces 90%–100% of Wave A (near but not exceeding)
Wave C = 1.0× Wave A; ends near the end of Wave A
More sideways than zigzag — less depth, more time
Found in: Wave 2, Wave 4, Wave B of larger ABC
Signal: trend will resume with similar strength on next motive wave
Regular Flat Structure

Expanded Flat (3-3-5)

B exceeds the start of A; C exceeds the end of A. The most common "count breaker."

Structure: A(3 waves) — B(3 waves) — C(5 waves)
Wave B exceeds 100% of Wave A — makes a NEW extreme (trap!)
Wave C = 1.236–1.618 × Wave A; makes a new extreme in opposite direction
Traders are trapped by the false B breakout before C reversal
B = 1.0–1.38 × A → expect expanded flat
C target: 1.618 × A from the B extreme
If B = 1.38+ × A → may be an irregular flat (see Irregular section)
Expanded Flat Structure

Running Flat (3-3-5)

B exceeds A start; C FAILS to reach the end of A. Indicates overwhelming trend strength.

Structure: A(3 waves) — B(3 waves) — C(5 waves)
Wave B exceeds 100% of Wave A (like expanded flat)
Wave C FAILS to reach the end of Wave A — trend is too strong
Most bullish/bearish corrective pattern — strong trend signal
C failing to reach A end confirms running flat vs expanded
Most common in Wave 4 — the trend wants to continue NOW
Explosive thrust follows — running flats precede the strongest Wave 3s
Running Flat Structure

Complex Corrections
Double Three · Triple Three · X Waves
ℹComplex corrections are sideways structures combining two or three simple corrections (zigzags, flats, triangles) connected by intervening X waves. They appear most often as Wave 4 or Wave B of a larger correction.

Double Three (W-X-Y)

Two simple corrective patterns connected by an X wave
W = first correction (zigzag, flat, or triangle)
X = connecting wave (3-wave rally/decline, typically 38.2–61.8% of W)
Y = second correction (any simple pattern, usually different from W)
The X wave often looks like a new impulse — don't be fooled
Combination guidelines: Zigzag-Flat, Flat-Triangle, Zigzag-Triangle (most common)
Time: usually takes 2–4× longer than a single correction

Triple Three (W-X-Y-X-Z)

Three simple corrective patterns connected by two X waves
W-X-Y-X-Z: extremely rare, very long-lasting sideways chop
Both X waves should be smaller and simpler than the corrections around them
Avoid trading inside triple threes — too unpredictable
Two adjacent zigzags = invalid (use double zigzag instead)
Triangle can ONLY appear as the final structure (Y or Z) — never W
The exit from a triple three is often explosive — patience rewarded

★ Irregular Waves
The Advanced Corrective Structures — Often Misread, Always Significant
★ What Are Irregular Waves? Irregular waves are corrective structures where the B wave travels BEYOND the start of Wave A, creating a false breakout that traps traders. They were originally classified by R.N. Elliott himself, though many practitioners only cover regular corrections. Mastering irregular patterns is essential for avoiding false signals and correctly identifying powerful continuation or reversal setups.

Irregular Flat (3-3-5)

The B wave significantly exceeds the start of Wave A, before a C wave decline that may or may not exceed the end of A.

★ The Defining Feature

Wave B travels beyond 100% (often 138%+) of Wave A, making a NEW extreme. This creates a "B wave trap" — traders buy/sell the breakout only to be caught by the reversal C wave.

Structure: A(3 waves) — B(3 waves, B > 100% of A) — C(5 waves)
Wave A sub-divides into 3 waves (NOT 5 — distinguishes from zigzag)
Wave B = 1.0–1.618 × Wave A (typically 1.236 or 1.382)
Wave C = 1.0–1.618 × Wave A; ends near or beyond Wave A's end
Fibonacci targets for C: 1.0, 1.236, 1.382, or 1.618 × A (measured from B extreme)
Found in: Wave 2, Wave 4, Wave B of a larger ABC structure
B wave looks like a resumption of trend — this is the trap!
Irregular Flat — Bullish Correction (In Uptrend)

Trading the Irregular Flat

  • Do NOT chase the B wave breakout — wait for sub-structure completion
  • B wave in 3 sub-waves → confirms flat family (irregular, regular, or expanded)
  • Enter C wave direction after B peak confirmed with momentum reversal
  • Target: C = 1.0–1.618 × A, measured from B extreme
  • Stop: beyond 1.618 × A from B extreme (would negate pattern)

Irregular Failure (Failed C / Truncated C)

The C wave fails to reach — or barely reaches — the end of Wave A, signaling extreme momentum in the primary trend direction.

★ The Defining Feature

Wave C fails to extend to the normal target of Wave A's end. This failure reveals that the underlying trend is so powerful that normal corrective forces cannot achieve their typical depth. Expect an explosive resumption of the primary trend.

Structure: A(3 or 5) — B(3 waves) — C(5 waves, FAILS to reach A end)
C is typically shorter than A — less than 61.8% of A in depth
B often travels 100%+ of A (making it also look like expanded flat briefly)
Signal: PRIMARY TREND WILL RESUME WITH EXCEPTIONAL FORCE
Also known as: "Truncated C", "C Failure", "Weak C"
Risk: if C continues past A end → not an irregular failure → recount
Best context: appears in Wave 4 of a powerful Wave 3 extension
Irregular Failure — In Uptrend (Bullish)

Why Irregular Failure is High Value

  • The failure of C to reach normal target confirms extreme trend strength
  • Entry: at the completion of the failed C wave (after 5-wave C structure visible)
  • Stop: below the A end level (if price reaches normal target, pattern invalidated)
  • Target: Wave 5 extension, often 1.618–2.618 × Wave 1 from Wave 4 end
  • RSI: will NOT reach oversold/overbought during the failure — another confirmation

Running Correction

The entire A-B-C corrective structure remains above (in uptrend) or below (in downtrend) the start of Wave A. The market barely corrects before resuming.

★ The Defining Feature

In a running correction, Wave B goes significantly beyond Wave A's start, and Wave C does NOT reach the end of Wave A — leaving the entire correction "above" the prior Wave A origin. This is the most bullish (or bearish) corrective structure possible.

B travels 100%+ beyond A start (like irregular flat)
C remains above Wave A's start point (the entire structure runs higher)
Extremely bullish: market refuses to correct even to the prior extreme
Structure can be: Running Zigzag (5-3-5) or Running Flat (3-3-5)
Context: Wave 2 or Wave 4 in an exceptionally powerful impulse
Often mistaken for a new impulse — careful, it's still corrective!
Confirm with sub-wave structure: A and C must be 3-wave or 5-wave per type
If C goes BELOW start of A → it's no longer a running correction → recount

How to Identify an Irregular Wave in Real-Time

  • Step 1: Identify a 3-wave (A wave) move against the primary trend
  • Step 2: Watch the B wave — if it exceeds the start of A by >100%, suspect irregular
  • Step 3: Count B sub-waves — must be 3 waves (zigzag or flat), NOT 5
  • Step 4: Measure B = 1.0–1.618 × A for irregular flat; >1.382 favors irregular
  • Step 5: Wait for B peak confirmed (momentum reversal, RSI divergence)
  • Step 6: Enter C wave trade after B confirmed. Count C sub-waves (must be 5)
  • Step 7: If C fails to reach A end → irregular failure → primary trend resuming now

Critical Difference: B Wave in 3 vs 5

The single most important confirmation of any flat-family pattern (including all irregulars) is that the B wave sub-divides into exactly 3 waves — NOT 5. A 5-wave B would indicate the start of a new impulse, not a corrective B wave. Always count B sub-waves before trading.

Irregular Wave Psychology

B wave breakout traps aggressive trend-followers who chase the "breakout"
C wave then reverses sharply, stopping out the B wave chasers
The real trade is FADING the B wave breakout after it completes
In irregular failure: even the C wave can't reach normal targets → shorts trapped in downtrend
Running correction: BOTH B and C are traps in the direction of correction — only trend traders win

The key to irregular waves is understanding they represent markets where the underlying trend is so dominant that corrective forces cannot achieve normal depth or extent. This is always a signal that the primary trend will resume with above-average force once the irregular structure completes.

📐 Arithmetic (Linear) Scale

Equal vertical distance = equal price change in absolute $ terms. $10→$20 looks same height as $110→$120.

✓ Use Arithmetic: Price range less than 2:1 (low to high)
✓ Use Arithmetic: Intraday charts (1min–4H) — price moves too small for log to matter
✓ Use Arithmetic: Any wave spanning less than 3× the starting price
✓ Use Arithmetic: Horizontal Fib retracement lines — always same on both
✓ Use Arithmetic: Minor/minute degree waves (hours to days)
✗ Arithmetic Distorts: When price has moved more than 100% from wave start
✗ Arithmetic Distorts: Long-term channels spanning decades
✗ Arithmetic Distorts: Commodity secular bulls (Gold $250→$5,500+)

📊 Logarithmic (Log) Scale

Equal vertical distance = equal PERCENTAGE change. $10→$20 (+100%) looks same height as $100→$200 (+100%).

✓ Use Log: Price has moved more than 100% (doubled) from chart start
✓ Use Log: Weekly and monthly charts spanning years or decades
✓ Use Log: Gold, Silver, Oil — commodity secular bull markets
✓ Use Log: Cryptocurrency — moves of 1,000%–10,000% are normal
✓ Use Log: W3 extension projections when W3 target is 2× starting price
✓ Use Log: Elliott Wave channels on primary and cycle degree waves
✗ Log Distorts: Narrow price ranges — log and arithmetic nearly identical
✗ Log Distorts: Horizontal Fibonacci retracement lines (same on both)
⚠Critical Distinction: Horizontal Fibonacci RETRACEMENT lines (0.382, 0.618, etc.) are mathematically IDENTICAL on both scales — they are simply price levels. Fibonacci EXTENSION projections and CHANNEL LINES are DIFFERENT on log vs arithmetic. Always draw channels on the same scale used for the wave analysis.

⚠RSI alone is not enough. Understanding when RSI gives misleading signals in EW context is as important as knowing the true signals. These are the most common RSI traps that cause traders to misread the wave count.

Trap 1 — Premature Divergence in Wave 3

During a powerful Wave 3, RSI may briefly pull back and create a false "bearish divergence" within the W3 sub-waves. This is NOT a real divergence — it's the sub-wave 4 correction within W3. True bearish divergence only counts between WAVE 3 and WAVE 5 peaks, not within Wave 3 itself.

Trap 2 — Wave B Looks Like Wave 3

In irregular and expanded flats, the B wave can push RSI to overbought (65–80+), making it look like a new Wave 3 impulse. The solution: count the sub-waves. If the "rally" only has 3 sub-waves, it's a B wave regardless of how high RSI goes. Always count before trading.

Trap 3 — Overbought in a Downtrend

In bear markets, RSI "overbought" means 50–60, NOT 70. Using bull-market RSI thresholds (70 = overbought) in a bear market will cause you to hold short too long and miss the Wave 2 bounce. Always calibrate OB/OS thresholds to the trend direction.

Trap 4 — Extended Wave 5 and No Divergence

When Wave 5 is itself extending (rare), RSI may actually EXCEED the Wave 3 reading briefly. This happens in commodity blow-offs. The solution: check Wave 5 sub-wave structure. If W5 is forming an ending diagonal internally, the divergence will appear within the diagonal's sub-waves even if not vs W3.

Trap 5 — Missing Irregular Failure

If you expect Wave C to reach OS on RSI and it doesn't (RSI stays 40–50 despite price declining), most traders wait for "more selling" — missing the actual reversal. Irregular Failure C is defined by RSI NOT reaching oversold. The failure to reach OS IS the signal.
